Stage 1: Becoming Aware

Many companies are not yet fully aware of how or where PFAS is used in their operations—or what the risks are. This section helps you better understand the issue and reflect on your role.

Do you know how PFAS is used in your company?

Let’s start by understanding what PFAS is and why it matters.

  • What are PFAS? A group of man-made chemicals used for water, oil, and heat resistance.
  • Why is this a problem? PFAS persist in the environment and in human bodies, causing health risks.
  • Where are they used? Products like coatings, packaging, wires, textiles, and electronics.

What role does your company want to play?

Reducing PFAS use isn’t just a regulatory issue — it’s about caring for public health, the environment, and future generations. As a company, you have a duty of care: to act responsibly and transparently when your actions may have harmful impacts.

Stage 2: Contemplation — Seeing the Benefit

At this stage, your company is aware of PFAS-related risks and is beginning to consider the benefits of eliminating them. This is the moment to build confidence and encourage motivation for real change.

Why Take Action?

  • Brand Reputation: Being PFAS-free sets your company apart as a sustainability leader.
  • Market Advantage: Consumers and clients increasingly demand non-toxic, responsible products.
  • Regulatory Readiness: Proactive adaptation protects you from future legal and environmental liabilities.

Techniques Used in This Stage:

  • Dramatic Relief: Understand the emotional and ethical weight of PFAS-related harm through case studies and testimonies.
  • Social Liberation: See how other businesses in your industry are making this change — and thriving.
  • Self-Reevaluation: Reflect on how this shift aligns with your company’s mission and long-term values.

First Practical Steps:

  • Start an internal discussion about PFAS and its risks — awareness is contagious.
  • Explore alternatives: What safer materials or suppliers are already available to you?
  • Identify a product, process, or packaging element that could serve as a PFAS-free pilot.
