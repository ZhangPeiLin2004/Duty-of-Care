Duty of Care

What does Duty of Care entail?

Promoting cleaner, safer practices isn’t just ethical—it’s increasingly required by law. “Duty of care” refers to the responsibility that companies, governments, and individuals have to prevent environmental harm and reduce health risks. This principle is central to European and international law and applies directly to PFAS use and pollution. Here's how your company can meet its duty of care obligations, structured around clear action categories:

1. Integrate into Operations

You are expected to incorporate environmental and human health considerations across your entire value chain. This means embedding responsibility for reducing harm—like PFAS pollution—into company policies and practices from top to bottom.

Legal Context: The Dutch Omgevingswet (Article 1.6) imposes a general duty on everyone to care for the physical environment. This reflects a shift toward “outsourced law,” where compliance is shaped by companies as much as legislators. Businesses are co-authors of environmental governance and must internalize sustainability.

2. Identify and Assess

Businesses are obligated to identify risks their activities pose to the environment, particularly those involving harmful substances like PFAS.

Legal Context: Articles 1.7 of the Omgevingswet and 9.2.1.2 of the Wet milieubeheer require preventive measures when harm is foreseeable. This is rooted in the precautionary principle and places the burden on companies to act in anticipation of risk.

3. Remediate

If environmental harm has occurred, those responsible are required to remediate the damage. This is not optional—it is a binding component of the duty of care.

Legal Context: Remediation duties stem from both statutory law and evolving jurisprudence. Even in the absence of explicit regulation, courts have affirmed that the general duty of care applies. This is particularly relevant in transnational issues like PFAS, where regulation may lag behind. Theoretically, duty of care fills regulatory gaps to ensure environmental protection remains enforceable.

4. Monitor

Duty of care is not static. Businesses must continuously monitor their processes and risks to remain compliant with evolving legal standards and scientific knowledge.

Legal Context: Monitoring obligations are supported by frameworks such as the EU Water Framework Directive. These require companies to keep pace with new scientific insights, particularly in relation to substances like PFAS. Legal scholars argue that this reflects a dynamic regulatory environment where actors are expected to adapt based on up-to-date knowledge and not wait for prescriptive rules.

5. Report

Transparency is essential. Companies must clearly report on risks, actions taken, and their results. This duty is not only strategic but increasingly enforceable.

Legal Context: Under regulations like the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD), companies are obligated to publicly disclose environmental and human rights impacts. Beyond compliance, legal philosophy underscores that public reporting facilitates democratic discourse and accountability—ensuring duty of care is not just private intent but publicly assessable practice.

PFAS Pioneers

Our Certification

Becoming a PFAS Pioneer means your organization is actively taking steps to reduce PFAS-related harm. This certification is grounded in the evolving concept of “duty of care” as understood in Dutch and European environmental law. It recognizes not just results, but intent, initiative, and leadership.

Engaged Contributor

This level recognizes organizations that are actively participating in PFAS-related awareness and are beginning to implement responsible practices. These organizations acknowledge their duty of care and are initiating internal assessments and stakeholder dialogue.

Significant Advancement

This level is awarded to organizations demonstrating measurable progress in reducing PFAS usage or emissions. These entities go beyond basic compliance by taking concrete steps based on contextual knowledge and risk analysis. Their actions reflect the legal principle that parties must prevent harm when reasonably foreseeable.

Exemplary Leader

This is the highest level of recognition, granted to organizations that are setting the benchmark in PFAS mitigation. These pioneers operationalize the full extent of duty of care by incorporating best practices across their value chain and engaging in transparent reporting and cross-sector leadership. They embody the forward-looking legal theory that emphasizes decentralized, proactive, and participatory regulation.

Section 1: Understanding your company's PFAS use

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are used in everything from textiles to semiconductors. They're also known as 'forever chemicals' — they do not break down naturally and accumulate in water, soil, and our bodies.

Framework for Assessing PFAS Impact

Suggestion: Begin with an inventory of products containing PFAS

Into watersystems? Air? Waste stream?

3. Identify who is being impacted
  • Local communities: Are any located near where PFAS might be discharged?
  • Workers: Are employees exposed during handling or disposal?
  • End users: Will customers be exposed?
  • Future generations: What long-term risks are created?
  • Ecosystems: Are rivers, groundwater, or wildlife potentially affected?

Suggestions for Getting Started:

  • Begin a PFAS Screening Audit
  • Review internal product specifications and supplier disclosures
  • Use publicly available tools (e.g., EPA's PFAS lists, NGO watchlists)

5. If no action is taken, could your company face litigation, product recalls, or loss of customer trust related to PFAS?

Important Note:

The answer is always YES, until you stop using PFAS completely!

Even if your company has not yet encountered legal claims or public scrutiny over PFAS, the risk is real and growing.

This is where duty of care becomes critical. In legal terms, duty of care is the responsibility an organization holds to prevent foreseeable harm. Courts are increasingly recognizing that companies must not only respond to known risks, but also anticipate and mitigate emerging threats — especially those with mounting scientific, regulatory, and societal concern, like PFAS.

If your company fails to act despite mounting evidence — or continues using PFAS without exploring safer alternatives — it may be argued that you breached your duty of care. This breach can form the basis for:

  • Lawsuits from customers, employees, or downstream businesses
  • Product recalls or bans under expanding chemical safety laws
  • Damage to brand trust, shareholder confidence, and customer loyalty

Even companies that did not intentionally cause harm can be held liable if they ignored clear warnings or failed to pursue reasonable precautions. Courts and regulators ask:

"Did the company take steps that a reasonable organization in its position should have taken?"

By taking even small steps now — such as evaluating PFAS use, exploring alternatives, or engaging suppliers — your company demonstrates proactive care and responsible governance. This not only reduces legal exposure but strengthens your standing with customers, investors, and regulators alike.

Inaction IS negligence.

How is it a business strategy?

Going PFAS-Free: A Strategic Business Move That Reshapes Industry Norms

Phasing out PFAS is not just a compliance or environmental issue — it is a strategic business decision with far-reaching implications. By demonstrating that your company can reduce or eliminate PFAS while maintaining quality, profitability, and innovation, you position yourself as a leader in responsible industry practice.

Importantly, this leadership creates legal and market leverage:

  • You set the benchmark for what is “reasonably possible.” Courts and regulators use the actions of peers to determine whether a company has met its duty of care. If your organization proves it is possible to operate with less or no PFAS, you raise the bar across the sector. Competitors who continue business as usual may no longer claim that phasing out PFAS is infeasible — because you’ve shown it can be done.
  • You shape the narrative of accountability. Public perception and legal risk increasingly center on which companies took initiative — and which waited to be forced. Early action strengthens your credibility and reduces your exposure to lawsuits, reputational damage, or investor withdrawal.
  • You build trust with customers, employees, and regulators. A proactive PFAS strategy shows that your company is not merely reacting to crisis, but thinking ahead. This empowers stakeholders to align with your brand, knowing they are choosing safety, sustainability, and leadership.
  • You gain early access to growing PFAS-free markets. As more consumers, retailers, and governments set PFAS restrictions, demand is shifting. Companies that are prepared can capture market share, meet certifications, and unlock new partnerships where others lag behind.

In short, eliminating PFAS isn't just about risk — it’s about competitive advantage. Your company's initiative doesn’t just protect your future — it helps shape what the future of your industry will be.
